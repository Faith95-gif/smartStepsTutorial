CHANGE OF SUBJECT OF FORMULAS

🎯 Introduction

Imagine you have a recipe for making jollof rice, but instead of knowing how much rice to use, you want to find out how much water you need. You'd have to rearrange the recipe formula to solve for water instead of rice.

This is exactly what changing the subject of a formula means - rearranging a formula to make a different variable the main focus (the subject).

This topic is crucial for JAMB Mathematics because it appears in algebra, physics formulas, geometry, and many word problems.

📖 What is Change of Subject of Formula?

Change of subject of formula means rearranging a mathematical formula to make a different variable stand alone on one side of the equation.

Key Point:

The subject of a formula is the variable that stands alone on one side of the equation (usually the left side).

Example:

In A = lw, the subject is A
If we change to w = Al, the subject becomes w

🔧 Basic Operations for Changing Subject

Golden Rule:

Whatever operation you do to one side of the equation, you must do the same to the other side!

Addition/Subtraction

If: x + a = b
Then: x = b - a

Move terms by changing signs

Multiplication/Division

If: ax = b
Then: x = ba

Move factors by dividing

Square/Square Root

If: x² = a
Then: x = ±√a

Take square root of both sides

Fractions

If: xa = b
Then: x = ab

Cross multiply

📝 Worked Examples

Example 1 — Simple Linear Formula

Problem: Make x the subject of the formula: y = 3x + 5

Solution:

Given formula: y = 3x + 5

Goal: Make x the subject

Step 1: Cube both sides to remove the cube root

P³ = [Q(R - T)15]
P³ = Q(R - T)15

Step 2: Multiply both sides by 15

15P³ = Q(R - T)

Step 3: Divide both sides by Q

15P³Q = R - T

Step 4: Solve for T

T = R - 15P³Q
T = R - 15P³Q

Problem: If xa + 1 + yb = 1, make y the subject of the relation

Solution:

Given formula: xa + 1 + yb = 1

Goal: Make y the subject

Step 1: Subtract xa + 1 from both sides

yb = 1 - xa + 1

Step 2: Express right side with common denominator

yb = a + 1a + 1 - xa + 1
yb = a + 1 - xa + 1

Step 3: Multiply both sides by b

y = b × a + 1 - xa + 1
y = b(a + 1 - x)a + 1

Problem: Make S the subject of the relation: p = s + sm²nr

Solution:

Given formula: p = s + sm²nr

Goal: Make s the subject

Step 1: Factor out s from the right side

p = s(1 + m²nr)

Step 2: Express the bracket with common denominator

p = s(nrnr + m²nr)
p = s(nr + m²nr)

Step 3: Divide both sides by (nr + m²nr)

s = p ÷ (nr + m²nr)
s = p × nrnr + m²
s = nrpnr + m²

mN = √SLT

m²T = N²SL

JAMB Question 7

Problem: If gt² - k - w = 0, make g the subject of the formula

Solution:

Given formula: gt² - k - w = 0

Goal: Make g the subject

Step 1: Add k and w to both sides

gt² - k - w + k + w = 0 + k + w
gt² = k + w

Step 2: Divide both sides by t²

gt²t² = k + wt²
g = k + wt²

JAMB Question 8

Problem: Make c the subject of the equation: a(b + c) + 5d - 2 = 0

Solution:

Given formula: a(b + c) + 5d - 2 = 0

Goal: Make c the subject

Step 1: Expand the brackets

ab + ac + 5d - 2 = 0

Step 2: Isolate the term with c

ac = 0 - ab - 5d + 2
ac = 2 - ab - 5d

Step 3: Express with common denominator

ac = 2d - abd - 5d

Step 4: Divide both sides by a

c = 2d - abd - 5ad
c = 2d - 5 - abdad

⚡ Quick Method Guide

Step-by-Step Approach:

  1. Identify the variable you want to make the subject
  2. Isolate terms containing that variable on one side
  3. Move all other terms to the opposite side
  4. Factor out the variable if it appears in multiple terms
  5. Divide by the coefficient of the variable
  6. Apply inverse operations (square root, reciprocal, etc.) if needed

Memory Tips:

  • BODMAS in reverse: Undo operations in opposite order
  • What crosses the equals sign changes sign (+/- operations)
  • What moves across becomes reciprocal (×/÷ operations)
  • Check your answer by substituting back into the original formula

🔍 Common Mistakes to Avoid

⚠️ Watch Out For:

  • Sign errors: Remember +5 becomes -5 when moved across equals
  • Reciprocal confusion: 1x = y means x = 1y, not x = y
  • Square root signs: √x² = |x|, consider positive and negative values
  • Factoring mistakes: Don't forget to factor out common terms
  • Order of operations: Follow BODMAS when simplifying
  • Fraction operations: Always find common denominators before adding/subtracting
  • Power operations: Remember (aᵐ)ⁿ = aᵐⁿ and aᵐ × aⁿ = aᵐ⁺ⁿ
