PROBABILITY

üéØ Introduction

Probability measures the likelihood of an event occurring. From weather forecasts to lottery tickets, probability helps us understand uncertainty and make informed decisions. In JAMB mathematics, probability questions often involve dice, cards, balls in boxes, and real-life scenarios.

üé≤ Basic Concepts

P(Event) = Number of Favorable OutcomesTotal Number of Possible Outcomes

Key Terms:

  • Experiment: An action with uncertain results (e.g., rolling a die)
  • Sample Space: All possible outcomes of an experiment
  • Event: A subset of the sample space
  • Probability Range: 0 ‚â§ P(Event) ‚â§ 1
P(A') = 1 - P(A)   (Complementary Event)
P(A ‚à™ B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A ‚à© B)   (Union)

Question 1 JAMB 2023

A die is thrown once. What is the probability of obtaining an even number?

Solution:

Sample Space: {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}

Even numbers: {2, 4, 6}

Total outcomes = 6
Favorable outcomes = 3 (numbers 2, 4, 6)
P(even) = 36
P(even) = 12

Question 2 JAMB 2022

A bag contains 5 red balls, 3 blue balls, and 2 green balls. If a ball is drawn at random, find the probability that it is not red.

Solution:

Total balls = 5 + 3 + 2 = 10
Red balls = 5
Non-red balls = 3 + 2 = 5

Method 1: Direct calculation

P(not red) = 510 = 12

Method 2: Using complement

P(red) = 510 = 12
P(not red) = 1 - P(red) = 1 - 12
P(not red) = 12

Question 4 JAMB 2020

A card is drawn from a standard deck of 52 cards. What is the probability of drawing a king or a heart?

Solution:

Let A = drawing a king, B = drawing a heart

Number of kings = 4

Question 5 JAMB 2019

A box contains 8 red balls and 4 blue balls. Two balls are drawn without replacement. Find the probability that both balls are red.

Solution:

Total balls = 8 + 4 = 12

For the first ball:

P(1st ball is red) = 812 = 23

For the second ball (given first is red):

Remaining red balls = 7, Total remaining = 11
P(2nd ball is red | 1st is red) = 711

Probability of both red:

P(both red) = P(1st red) √ó P(2nd red | 1st red)
P(both red) = 23 √ó 711
P(both red) = 1433

Question 7 JAMB 2017

A coin is tossed 3 times. Find the probability of getting at least one head.

Solution:

Method 1: Using complement

P(at least one head) = 1 - P(no heads) = 1 - P(all tails)
P(tail on one toss) = 12
P(all tails in 3 tosses) = (12)¬≥ = 18
P(at least one head) = 1 - 18 = 78

Method 2: Direct counting

Sample space: {HHH, HHT, HTH, HTT, THH, THT, TTH, TTT}
Events with at least one head: All except TTT = 7 outcomes
P(at least one head) = 78

Question 8 JAMB 2016

From the digits 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, a three-digit number is formed without replacement. Find the probability that the number is even.

Solution:

Total three-digit numbers without replacement:

First digit: 5 choices
Second digit: 4 choices (one used)
Third digit: 3 choices (two used)
Total numbers = 5 √ó 4 √ó 3 = 60

Even numbers (last digit must be 2 or 4):

Case 1: Last digit is 2
First digit: 4 choices (1,3,4,5), Second digit: 3 choices
Numbers ending in 2: 4 √ó 3 = 12
Case 2: Last digit is 4
Numbers ending in 4: 4 √ó 3 = 12
Total even numbers = 12 + 12 = 24
P(even) = 2460 = 25
P(even number) = 25

Question 11 JAMB 2013

If P(A) = 0.6, P(B) = 0.4, and P(A ‚à© B) = 0.2, find P(A' ‚à© B').

Solution:

Using De Morgan's Law: A' ‚à© B' = (A ‚à™ B)'

P(A ‚à™ B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A ‚à© B)
P(A ‚à™ B) = 0.6 + 0.4 - 0.2 = 0.8
P(A' ‚à© B') = P((A ‚à™ B)') = 1 - P(A ‚à™ B)
P(A' ‚à© B') = 1 - 0.8
P(A' ‚à© B') = 0.2

Question 15 JAMB 2009

In a group of 50 people, 30 like tea, 25 like coffee, and 10 like both. If a person is selected at random, find the probability that the person likes neither tea nor coffee.

Question 16 JAMB 2008

A fair die is rolled 4 times. Find the probability of getting exactly 2 sixes.

Solution:

This is a binomial probability problem:

n = 4 (number of trials)
r = 2 (number of successes needed)
p = 16 (probability of success on one trial)
q = 1 - p = 56 (probability of failure)

Using P(X = r) = C(n,r) √ó pr √ó qn-r:

P(X = 2) = C(4,2) √ó (16)¬≤ √ó (56)¬≤
C(4,2) = 4!2!2! = 6
P(X = 2) = 6 √ó 136 √ó 2536
P(X = 2) = 6 √ó 251296 = 1501296
P(exactly 2 sixes) = 25216

üí° Memory Tips for JAMB Success

The Addition Rule

P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A and B)
Don't forget to subtract the intersection to avoid double counting!

Complement Rule

P(not A) = 1 - P(A)
Sometimes it's easier to find the probability of what you DON'T want!

With vs Without Replacement

With replacement: Probabilities stay the same
Without replacement: Probabilities change after each draw
