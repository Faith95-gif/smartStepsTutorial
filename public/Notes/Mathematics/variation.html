VARIATION

üéØ Introduction

Variation describes how one quantity changes in relation to another. In everyday life, we see variation everywhere - as the speed of a car increases, the time to reach a destination decreases. As the number of workers on a project increases, the time to complete it decreases. Understanding variation is crucial for solving real-world problems in JAMB mathematics.

üìê Types of Variation

1. Direct Variation (Direct Proportion)

When one quantity increases, the other increases proportionally.

y ‚àù x or y = kx

Example: Distance traveled is directly proportional to time (at constant speed).

2. Inverse Variation (Inverse Proportion)

When one quantity increases, the other decreases proportionally.

y ‚àù 1x or y = kx

Example: Time to complete a task is inversely proportional to number of workers.

3. Joint Variation

When one quantity varies directly with the product of two or more quantities.

z ‚àù xy or z = kxy

4. Partial Variation

When one quantity is partly constant and partly varies with another.

y = a + bx
